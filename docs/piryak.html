Like a Dragon: Pirate Yakuza in Hawaii

Like a Dragon: Pirate Yakuza in Hawaii is a game just about insane as the name may suggest. Even for a Yakuza game, a series well known for not abiding to most standards of logic or reasoning, is perhaps the wildest and most out-there entries in the series- and in a good way. Playing as Goro Majima, who washes up in Hawaii without his memory, and remakes his life as a pirate. The fundamental design in this game, even compared to other Yakuza games, stands out in the way it avoids trying too hard to tell a story, and just does whatever it feels is fun. One moment you can be defusing a hostage situation, then having the free will to play baseball with cannonballs and exploding barrels to buy the angry birds theme. And when you're done with that, since you're short on cash, you hop on your pirate ship with, destroying enemy ship with your pink and green lasers, and board it with a crew of people you recruited by beating up, giving them something from a claw machine, or giving them a single beer. Needless to say, trying to give an understandable summary of this game to anyone who hasn't played it would take longer than it takes me to actually upload these articles. The amount you can do in this game, even for a smaller scale and shorter game compared to the rest of the Yakuza series, is extensive and can keep you busy for hours. And a lot of this is encouraged for you to do whenever you want, there is rarely a point where you are forced to progress the story, nor does it try to make it feel necessary to continue, whether you view that as a good or bad thing.

Even for a game on a smaller scale, the detail put into Pirate Yakuza is extensive, going far as making an accurate rendition of the city of Honolulu, putting in plenty of landmarks and copyright-safe buildings that feeds your urge to explore. The only outstandingly possible inaccuracy is the mildly exaggerated street fights you engage in every ten seconds, but as a staple of the Yakuza series, it's not exactly something you either can't handle or don't enjoy. Perhaps an even more interesting feature is the naval combat, because Pirate isn't in the title for no reason. The open world extends out to the high seas, where you can fight various groups of pirates, all the way to the pirate haven of Madlantis, a section of the game so vastly different I would need to double the length of this review to explain its relevance and functions. As a whole, Pirate Yakuza is certainly a one of a kind entry in a one of a kind series, where there is always one more thing for you to do, and it never fails to excite. Except for Mahjong, I still don't understand how to play it and never will. Overall this game is an excellent piece from the Yakuza series that sacrifices a more serious story for an experience you will remember.
